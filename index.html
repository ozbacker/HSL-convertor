Understanding Digital and Physical Color Mathematics

Color spaces are mathematical structures created to represent colors systematically using numeric coordinates. Depending on whether your project targets screens, broadcast video, or industrial printers, choosing the correct color architecture changes how light properties translate across computer logic arrays.

The Core Color Models Explained

Space Primary Dimensions Primary Medium
RGB Red, Green, Blue Digital Displays & Emissive Light
HSL Hue, Saturation, Lightness UI Styling & Intuitive Design Mapping
CMYK Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, Key (Black) Physical Paper Ink Subtractive Printing
YUV Luma (Y), Chroma Blue (U), Chroma Red (V) Television Signals & Video Compression

Additive vs. Subtractive Systems

Digital display arrays (RGB) run on additive light mixtures. When there is no signal, the pixel element remains absolute black. Mixing all three values at maximum power results in pure white light. On the flip side, physical printing presses use a subtractive ink model (CMYK). Unprinted paper reflects white light; applying pure Cyan, Magenta, and Yellow ink absorbs components of that light, shifting the final color toward black.
